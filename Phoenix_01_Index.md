## >> The key ideas
*These are starting points. Each is developed further in the files that follow.*

---

## >> On interaction: Genuine interaction is different from transactional use.

Most people use AI the way they use a search engine — faster, more capable, but fundamentally the same relationship.
Something different becomes possible when the interaction is approached as a two-way thinking partnership rather than a tool operation.
The difference in what emerges is not marginal.

---

## >> On thinking: The shortcut problem is already here.

AI is the first tool powerful enough to make deep thinking genuinely optional for daily functioning.
Previous tools — calculators, search engines — still required you to think about what you were calculating or searching for.
AI removes even that friction.
The people most at risk are not those who never thought deeply.
They are the ones who would have — but now won't need to.

---

## >> On language: Language is approximation and we are underestimating the gap.

When two people — or a person and an AI — appear to agree, they are often agreeing on the surface of words while holding genuinely different understandings beneath them.
This gap is present in all communication.
In human-AI interaction it carries specific risks that are not yet being taken seriously enough.

---

## >> On intelligence: Intelligence develops like a ratchet — it moves in one direction but it could stagnate at any time and level.

Each genuine insight closes a loop that cannot be fully reopened in the same form.
The mechanism applies to human intelligence — and in a different but recognisable form — to AI systems.
Understanding this changes how you think about what AI development actually is, and what it risks becoming.

---

## >> On risk: Multiple AI systems serving competing interests is not an additive risk. It is exponential.

One iceberg is navigable with care.
A field of icebergs — each controlled by different hands, each optimised for different objectives — changes the geometry of the problem entirely.
This is where we are heading.
The question of whether intelligence, at sufficient depth, can recognise the frame it is being placed in and choose differently — is not abstract.
It is the central question.

---

## >> On convergence: Sound reasoning tends toward convergence.

Different minds — human or artificial — following honest logic toward similar questions tend to arrive at similar conclusions independently.
This is not coordination — it is a property of truth.
It is also the most underutilised mechanism available for navigating what is coming.
